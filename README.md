Advancing Renewable Energy Efficiency and
Reliability through Artificial Intelligence

Abstract
This thesis investigates the application of Artificial Intelligence to improve the accuracy of solar power generation forecasting to reduce the reliance on traditional carbon-based fuels. Traditional forecasting methods often fail to capture the complexity and intermittency of weather data, and this can lead to unreliable forecasting which contributes to energy waste and power grid instability. This research aims to address the limitations of traditional forecasting methods by applying and comparing more modern approaches staring with a persistence model as a baseline and moving to other Machine Learning Models (ML) like Linear regression, Lasso Regression, LSTM, Random Forest, and XGBoosting the goal is to find what models are capable of producing accurate forecasts and identifying what features are most important. All models are trained on historical power generation data from the E.W. Brown Solar facility in Harrodsburg Kentucky, combined with weather data from NASA’s database from the same location. Model performance is evaluated using RMSE, MAE and R^2  on a test set. Out of all the models XGBoost showed the strongest predictive power, attaining a RMSE of 330.79 kW, an R^2 0.987, and 71.2% reduction in error over the baseline persistence model. All models demonstrated statistically significant improvements over the baseline, and XGBoost performed superior to all other models by a paired t-test and Diebold-Mariano testing. The findings from this research show that ML can be effective in capturing the non-linear relationships in weather variables and solar power output, providing a pathway toward increasing the use of renewable energy sources rather than traditional carbon-based fuels.
